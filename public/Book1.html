LEO TOLSTOY

"The Kingdom of God Is Within You" is a powerful essay by Leo Tolstoy, rejecting militarism and advocating for a society founded on principles of peace, freedom, and love derived from Jesus's teachings. Tolstoy, drawing from his own experiences in the Crimean War, criticizes the Christian Church's hypocrisy in supporting violence while professing Christ's message. He explores the history of non-resistance to evil, acknowledging precedents set by groups like the Quakers. Ultimately, the essay serves as a call to reevaluate societal values and embrace a more compassionate and nonviolent approach to conflict resolution.
"The Three Hermits" is a short story by Leo Tolstoy that follows a bishop's encounter with three humble hermits living on a remote island. Despite their lack of education and understanding of formal religious practices, the hermits possess a deep, genuine faith in God. The bishop, initially intrigued by their simplicity, attempts to teach them the correct way to pray. However, he soon realizes that their earnest devotion transcends mere words and rituals. As the story unfolds, the bishop witnesses a miraculous display of their faith when the hermits walk on water to seek his guidance once more. In this poignant tale, Tolstoy explores themes of humility, spirituality, and the true essence of faith, emphasizing that genuine devotion to God lies in the heart rather than in formalities or intellectual knowledge.
"Resurrection" follows Dmitri Nekhlyudov, a Russian nobleman, who tries to right the wrong he committed by having an affair with his maid, Katusha Maslova. Maslova ends up in a downward spiral, wrongly accused of murder and sentenced to hard labor. Nekhlyudov, plagued by guilt, fights for her release, but despite his efforts, he cannot fully redeem himself or win her love. The novel exposes the injustices of the legal system and the harsh realities faced by the lower classes in Tsarist Russia, while exploring themes of guilt, redemption, and social justice.
"War and Peace" by Leo Tolstoy is a sweeping epic that follows the lives of several aristocratic families in Russia during the early 19th century. Against the backdrop of Napoleon's invasion of Russia in 1812, the novel delves into the personal struggles, romances, and moral dilemmas faced by characters such as Pierre Bezukhov, Andrey Bolkonsky, and Nikolay Rostov. As war and peace alternate, the characters confront their own desires and responsibilities, weaving a rich tapestry of human experience amidst the tumultuous events of history.
"Childhood" follows Nicolas, a child from an aristocratic family in Russia, as he navigates his idyllic life on a country estate before moving to Moscow due to financial concerns. The story explores universal childhood experiences like friendship and love amidst the backdrop of aristocratic society. While praised for its portrayal of childhood psychology, the plot's cohesion is criticized until its powerful conclusion. Despite its shortcomings, "Childhood" offers valuable insights into Tolstoy's life and serves as a precursor to his later acclaimed works.

“A Confession” is a book written by Leo Tolstoy, in which he describes his spiritual journey and the personal crisis that led him to question the meaning of life and the traditional religious beliefs of his time. The book is an autobiography, and in it Tolstoy describes his struggles with faith, morality, and the search for true happiness. He ultimately comes to the conclusion that true happiness can only be found through living a simple, moral life and helping others. The book is considered a classic of Russian literature and one of Tolstoy’s most important works.
